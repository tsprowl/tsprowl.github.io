Final Project

My original plan had been to implement the boids algorithm, perhaps having the boids change color as they moved across the screen with some obstacles for them to fly around. However, I didn't really have much passion for this idea, and so I struggled to find something that I would find engaging and interesting. I let the projects sit for a bit, and eventually came back to this with a fresh mind. Over the course of this year, I have leaned more and more heavily into music as a way to fill my time, and I knew that I wanted to do something with music integrated with the shader.

Shortly after this realization, I ended up doing more work on the physarum algorithm, and immediately fell in love with how cool it looked. Compared to the other algorithms we did, it moved so smoothly and elegantly and I honestly just loved it so much. That was when it clicked for me: why not combine music with the physarum algorithm?

That is exactly what I've done in my shader project, nicknamed Physarum, Physarum, Physarum. It's a physarum simulation that has a standard set of values which are overridden by one of the song selections, which are done by clicking the TweakPane buttons. I originally planned it to just use music from Beetlejuice: The Musical (as evidenced by the nickname), but added in a couple of songs from Heathers: The Musical to add a bit of variety. Apart from variety, the songs used were chosen because they could easily have a mold-themed pun made out of them.

From Beetlejuice we have The Whole Being Dead Thing, Creepy Old Guy, and Dead Mom. From Heathers we have My Dead Gay Son and Our Love is God. The original color scheme still remains, the black background with white agents meant to resemble Beetlejuice's suit. Technically speaking, the lower frequencies modify the sensor distance for the agents, and the higher ones modify the rotation angle of the agents, leading to some interesting results. It's mostly sound, although there is a current issue where the audio will not play on Chromium-based browsers, resulting in the simulation not functioning properly.

The simulation does work within Firefox, however, so I recommend opening it with Firefox to get the best, and really only, experience.
